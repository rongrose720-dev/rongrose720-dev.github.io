Moving Forward Amid Uncertainty

It was at this very turning point
that changes also unfolded within my family and church.
These events did not give me clear answers,
but they led me to a realizationâ€”
I care too much about outcomes.

This is not a judgment of others,
but an understanding of myself.

When I invest, wait, and endure,
I am quietly hoping for a conclusionâ€”
one that proves this road is right,
one that reassures me I have not chosen wrongly.
And when reality does not move in that direction,
I begin to lose certainty
and seriously consider giving up.

I realized that
I was not questioning whether God is real,
but whether this road is still worth walking
if it does not produce the outcome I expect.

In this struggle,
I began to see more clearly
what God truly values.

God does not prioritize results,
but whether a person understands
their own redemption before Him.
Saving souls is a responsibility,
but one must not lose
the salvation of being redeemed
in the process of carrying that responsibility.

I often think of Moses.
People regret that he never entered Canaan,
but God wanted him to understand:
your destination is not Canaan,
but eternity.

This realization slowly turned inward.
The path my partner and I walk
may indeed feel surrounded on all sides.
Family situations remain complex,
and circumstances have not immediately improved.
Yet this does not mean
the road has drifted from Godâ€™s will.

I do not need to fully understand every part of this path,
nor do I need to see the end.
What I can do
is choose, each day, to keep moving forwardâ€”
to rely on Him amid uncertainty.

Along the way,
I began to notice a different order.
No matter how family or church circumstances shift,
no matter how reality tests my capacity,
faith is not meant to immediately resolve problems.
It is the ability to keep walking
while the problems remain.

So my partner and I are learning
to hold one another in our weakness.
In moments of loneliness and pressure,
we remind each other again and again:
the road still needs to be walked,
but fear and anxiety do not get to decide our steps.

This road may be difficult,
even doubtful at times,
but it is not aimless drifting.
It is a path that belongs to usâ€”
and a journey
walked with God.
